Understanding Climate Change Through Probability

This project applies probability and statistical methods to analyze real-world climate change data. By visualizing key trends—such as global temperature rise, carbon dioxide emissions, and sea level changes—I explored how statistical tools can uncover the patterns driving climate change.

Using bar graphs, line charts, pie charts, and histograms, I identified distributions, calculated probabilities, and made predictions based on historical and current climate data. This helped me understand how we can model environmental uncertainties and support data-driven decisions about our planet's future.

Scroll through the sections to explore how probability deepens our understanding of climate change.

Bar Graph

This bar chart illustrates the average land temperature for various countries, with temperatures ranging from 5°C to 27°C. Each bar represents a country, showing variations in average temperatures. For example, countries like Africa and Asia have higher average temperatures (around 25°C), while others like Antarctica have lower averages (around 5°C).

Line Chart

This time series graph displays average land temperature from 1750 to 2000, with temperatures fluctuating between -10°C and 30°C. The data shows periodic variations but an overall upward trend, particularly noticeable from the 1900s onward, suggesting a general increase in temperatures over the centuries.

Histogram

This histogram displays the frequency distribution of temperature values, with the x-axis labeled as "Value" (likely representing temperature anomalies or deviations in °C) and the y-axis showing frequency. The data forms a roughly bell-shaped curve, indicating that most temperature values cluster around the mean (near 0), with fewer occurrences of extreme values on either side.

Pie Chart

This pie chart represents the proportional distribution of average temperatures across different countries. Each segment corresponds to a country, with percentages indicating their contribution to the total temperature data. For example, Africa accounts for 9.4%, while smaller segments like Anguilla contribute 0.9%, showing the relative temperature impact of each country.

Box Plot

This box plot shows the distribution of average land temperatures for countries starting with the letter "A". The median temperature is 18.12°C, with the interquartile range (IQR) spanning from 11.20°C (Q1) to 24.67°C (Q3). Outliers are marked as individual points, indicating some countries with temperatures significantly higher or lower than the typical range for this group.

Binomial Distribution

This graph compares the empirical cumulative distribution function (CDF) of average temperatures (blue dots and line) with theoretical CDFs for Poisson (green), Binomial (blue line), and Normal (red) distributions. The empirical CDF shows the actual cumulative probability of temperatures, while the theoretical curves estimate how well these distributions fit the data. The Binomial distribution aligns closely with the empirical data, suggesting that the temperature distribution may follow a binomial pattern.

Linear Regression

This scatter plot with a linear regression line tracks average land temperature from 1750 to 2000. The regression line (y = 0.00x + 18.48, R² = 0.0389) shows a slight upward trend, indicating a gradual increase in temperature over time. The low R² value suggests significant variability in the data, but the trend predicts a small rise in average temperature.

Conclusion

The analysis of the climate data, particularly the scatter plot with linear regression, predicts a slight increase in average land temperature over time. The regression line (y = 0.00x + 18.48, R² = 0.0389) indicates a gradual rise, though the trend is weak due to significant variability in the data. This suggests that global temperatures may continue to rise slowly in the future if current patterns persist.
